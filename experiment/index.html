Participant Information Sheet

A study investigating methods for enhancing user performance and experience in human-computer interaction tasks.

Thank you for your interest in this study. Before you decide to take part, it is important for you to understand why the research is being done and what it will involve. Please take time to read the following information carefully and discuss it with others if you wish. The study organiser is available if there is anything that is not clear or if you would like more information. Take time to decide whether or not you wish to take part.

Purpose of the study

This study aims to investigate how user performance and experience in computer-based tasks can be improved by novel interface or interaction features. The specific details of the study will be explained by the study organiser but the tasks you will undertake will involve some combination of: (i) transcribing, composing or editing text or code; (ii) assessing the quality of system-produced suggestions, explanations or task-assistance; and (iii) providing feedback in relation to presented images, video, audio or text. As you complete these tasks, we will log data reflecting how well the system supported you in executing the task, what interaction behaviours you exhibited, and details about your subjective user experience. At the end of the study, you may also be asked to reflect upon and compare your experience of the different tasks performed and interfaces experienced. Your participation in this study will contribute to a better understanding of user needs and behaviours, and inform human-computer interaction design more broadly. Further details are provided below.

1. Why have I been chosen?

We aim to recruit a diverse range of participants to study user performance and experience in relation to computer-based tasks. No specialised background is typically required. However, in some cases we may specifically recruit participants with a particular background (e.g. existing knowledge of a topic or prior task experience) to examine how this influences task performance and experience. If relevant, this will be clearly stated in the call for participants or explained by the study organiser.

2. Do I have to take part?

No, participation is entirely voluntary. If you do decide to take part, you can still withdraw from the study at any time, without giving any reasons.

3. Who is organising the study?

Dr John Dudley is the Principal Investigator for this research study. He is based in the Department of Engineering at the University of Cambridge.

4. What will happen during the study?

You will be introduced to the study procedure and the tasks that you will be asked to perform. You may be asked to provide basic demographic data (e.g. age, gender, prior experience) but you may decline to provide this information if you wish. You will then be asked to complete the specified tasks during which performance data (e.g. task completion time) and interaction data (e.g. button click sequence) will be logged. At various intervals, you may also be asked questions regarding your experience of executing the tasks. This may include providing feedback, rating aspects of the task or system, and completing questionnaires regarding perceived workload (NASA-TLX), usability (SUS), user experience (UEQ), understanding (MMS), and creativity (CSI).

5. What are the possible risks of taking part?

The study will be completed on a computer, laptop, or smartphone, and there are no significant risks associated with participation. You may experience mild fatigue from looking at the screen, but you are free to take breaks at any time.

6. What happens at the end of the study?

At the end of the study, you may receive a short debrief. You may also ask any questions that you have. If you subsequently have questions, please feel free to contact the study organiser.

7. What will happen to the study results?

The anonymised results will be written up and published in academic papers and presented at research meetings. Collected data will be securely stored.

8. Anonymity – will I be identified in any publications resulting from the study?

The data will be anonymised and any references to the data will be done so that you are not identifiable.

9. What if there is a problem?

If you have a concern about any aspect of this study, you should ask to speak to the study organiser who will do their best to answer your questions.

10. Who has reviewed the study?

The Department of Engineering Ethics Committee has reviewed this study through the light-touch review process.

Important information about this study

The AI assistant you interacted with was not a real AI. This study used a Wizard-of-Oz technique: the responses were pre-scripted by the researchers. This approach was necessary to ensure that all participants in the same experimental condition received identical responses, allowing us to test the effect of the responses in a controlled way.

What the study was about

This study investigates whether personalised versus generic chatbot responses influence how inspired users feel and how motivated they are to act. The study is part of research conducted at the University of Cambridge (MLMI16).
